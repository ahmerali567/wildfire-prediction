## 📌 Project Overview
Current wildfire prediction models often rely on deterministic outputs that fail to capture the chaotic and stochastic nature of fire progression. This project fills that "Uncertainty Gap" by implementing a **Conditional Diffusion Model**. By integrating **GOES-16/17 satellite imagery** with real-time **Wind Vectors (Speed & Direction)** from the Open-Meteo API, the system generates multiple plausible fire spread scenarios rather than a single fixed guess.

## 🛠️ Data Preprocessing & Pipeline
The success of the Diffusion model relies on high-quality data synchronization. Our preprocessing pipeline consists of several critical steps:

### 1. Spatial & Temporal Alignment
*   **Coordinate Mapping**: Synchronized raw **GOES-16 radiance data** with **NASA FIRMS (MODIS/VIIRS)** active fire points using geographic-to-pixel coordinate transformations.
*   **Data Merging**: Aggregated millions of fire records from the 2024 Canada wildfire season into a unified timeline of events (`merged_canada_fires.csv`).

### 2. Weather Intelligence Integration
*   **API-Driven Fetching**: For every fire patch, we dynamically fetched historical wind data (speed and direction) via the **Open-Meteo API**.
*   **Wind Vectorization**: Converted wind direction (degrees) into **Sine/Cosine components**. This allows the model to process directional geometry continuously.

### 3. Image Preparation & Normalization
*   **Patch Extraction**: Cropped high-resolution satellite imagery into **256x256 pixel patches** focused on active fire fronts.
*   **Scaling**: Applied Min-Max normalization to scale radiance values to a range of $[0, 1]$.
*   **Visual Inspection**: Utilized `inspect_patch.py` to verify that **darker textures (high-radiance hotspots)** correctly align with active fire locations.

---

## 🏗️ Architecture Design

*   **Backbone**: A **Conditional U-Net** featuring a bottleneck Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) for weather injection.
*   **Stochastic Engine**: Employs **Gaussian Noise injection** ($\sigma = 0.05$) during the denoising phase to generate varied but physically grounded scenarios.
*   **Loss Convergence**: Achieved a final **MSE Training Loss of 0.0032**, demonstrating superior precision over standard CNN baselines.

🔍 Understanding the Output
In the provided results, the darker spots represent high-radiance wildfire hotspots. Unlike traditional models that offer one static prediction, our model generates 5 distinct "Ensemble Members," allowing disaster responders to visualize the Best Case, Worst Case, and Most Likely Case for fire progression based on current wind dynamics.
